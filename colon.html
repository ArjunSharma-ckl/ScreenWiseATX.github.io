Colorectal Cancer

Starting at age 45, everyone should be screened for colorectal cancer. Tests range from at-home stool kits to colonoscopy, and you can choose the option that fits your risk, comfort, and access.

  • Screen earlier if you have a family history or inflammatory bowel disease
  • Choose between stool-based tests every 1-3 years or colonoscopies every 10 years
  • Positive stool tests must be followed by a colonoscopy
*Who is average vs. higher risk?
  • Average risk: no personal/family history of colorectal cancer or advanced polyps, no inflammatory bowel disease, and no hereditary syndromes (like Lynch)
  • Higher risk: strong family history, personal history of polyps/IBD, or known genetic syndrome—start earlier and screen more often

Stool-Based Tests

These tests check stool for blood or DNA/RNA changes linked to colorectal cancer. They are non-invasive, at-home, and more frequent than visual exams.

  • Common types: FIT, gFOBT, multitarget stool DNA/RNA tests
  • Begin at age 45; reduce frequency around 75; stop near 85 unless advised otherwise
Test Type Procedure Screening Frequency
Fecal immunochemical test (FIT) Checks for hidden blood in stool; at home; no dietary restrictions. Every year from 45; colonoscopy if positive.
Guaiac-based fecal occult blood test (gFOBT) Looks for hidden blood; at home; drug/diet restrictions; less specific than FIT. Every year from 45; colonoscopy if positive.
Multitarget stool DNA/RNA tests Screens for mutated DNA/RNA plus blood; at home; no drug/diet restrictions. Popular: Cologuard. Every 3 years from 45; colonoscopy if positive.

Visual Exams

These exams look directly at the colon and rectum for cancer or polyps and can remove polyps during the procedure. They are less frequent but more involved than stool tests.

  • Types: Colonoscopy, CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy), Sigmoidoscopy
Test Type Procedure Screening Frequency
Colonoscopy Tube with light/camera via anus into lower intestine; biopsies possible; special diet/fasting before; can be uncomfortable. Every 10 years from 45; genetic/high-risk may need every 1–5 years.
CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy) X-rays/CT take 3D pictures of rectum/colon; no sedation; catheter placed in rectum; less invasive; special diet/fasting before. Often every 5 years for average risk; follow clinician guidance.
Sigmoidoscopy Scope examines rectum and lower colon; prep required; quicker than colonoscopy. Typically every 5 years for average risk; follow clinician guidance.

FIT or FOBT

Annual stool tests (FIT or FOBT) look for hidden blood. They’re inexpensive, done at home, and a great first step if you’re average risk.

DNA Stool Tests

Every 3 years, multi-target stool DNA tests (like Cologuard) check for abnormal cells plus blood, offering more sensitivity from your couch.

Colonoscopy

A colonoscopy views the entire colon and removes polyps during the same visit. Repeat every 10 years if normal, or sooner if polyps are found.

Other Imaging

CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy) every 5 years is an option if you prefer imaging, but abnormal results still require a traditional colonoscopy.

Signs to Discuss with a Clinician

  • Blood in stool or persistent changes in bowel habits
  • Unexplained weight loss or abdominal cramping
  • Iron-deficiency anemia without another cause
