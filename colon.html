Colorectal Cancer

Starting at age 45, everyone should be screened for colorectal cancer. Tests range from at-home stool kits to colonoscopy, and you can choose the option that fits your risk, comfort, and access.

  • Screen earlier if you have a family history or inflammatory bowel disease
  • Choose between stool-based tests every 1-3 years or colonoscopies every 10 years
  • Positive stool tests must be followed by a colonoscopy
*Who is average vs. higher risk?
  • Average risk: no personal/family history of colorectal cancer or advanced polyps, no inflammatory bowel disease, and no hereditary syndromes (like Lynch)
  • Higher risk: strong family history, personal history of polyps/IBD, or known genetic syndrome—start earlier and screen more often

FIT or FOBT

Annual stool tests (FIT or FOBT) look for hidden blood. They’re inexpensive, done at home, and a great first step if you’re average risk.

DNA Stool Tests

Every 3 years, multi-target stool DNA tests (like Cologuard) check for abnormal cells plus blood, offering more sensitivity from your couch.

Colonoscopy

A colonoscopy views the entire colon and removes polyps during the same visit. Repeat every 10 years if normal, or sooner if polyps are found.

Other Imaging

CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy) every 5 years is an option if you prefer imaging, but abnormal results still require a traditional colonoscopy.

Signs to Discuss with a Clinician

  • Blood in stool or persistent changes in bowel habits
  • Unexplained weight loss or abdominal cramping
  • Iron-deficiency anemia without another cause
