Cervical Cancer

HPV awareness Preventable cancer

Cervical cancer begins in the cells of the cervix. Most cases are caused by certain strains of human papillomavirus (HPV), which makes vaccination and routine screening powerful tools for prevention.

What is it?

Cervical cancer forms in the cells lining the cervix, where the uterus connects to the vagina. Persistent infection with high-risk HPV types is the main cause.

Pap smears and HPV tests can find abnormal cells long before they turn into cancer.

Who is most affected?

  • People with a cervix, especially ages 35-44
  • Anyone with multiple sexual partners or a partner with many partners
  • People with weakened immune systems
  • Smokers

Risk factors

  • HPV infection (types 16 and 18 cause most cases)
  • Smoking or secondhand smoke exposure
  • Long-term birth control pill use (5+ years)
  • Chlamydia infection
  • Multiple full-term pregnancies or early first pregnancy
  • Family history of cervical cancer
  • Exposure to DES before birth

Symptoms—call your doctor if you notice:

  • Vaginal bleeding after intercourse, between periods, or after menopause
  • Watery or bloody discharge with possible odor
  • Pelvic pain or pain during intercourse
  • Painful urination or persistent pelvic/back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss or fatigue

Prevention

  • HPV vaccination starting at ages 9-12 (catch-up through 26; discuss 27-45)
  • Routine Pap/HPV testing as recommended
  • Condom use and limiting tobacco exposure
