Cervical Cancer

What is it?

Cervical cancer is a type of cancer that occurs in the cells of the cervix — the lower part of the uterus that connects to the vagina. Most cervical cancers are caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV).

It is most common in (but not limited to)...

  • Women between the ages of 35 and 44
  • Women who have had multiple sexual partners or have a partner who has had multiple partners
  • Women who have a weakened immune system
  • Women who smoke
  • Women who have given birth to three or more children
  • Women who have used birth control pills for five or more years

Risk Factors

Factors that can increase your risk of cervical cancer include:

  • HPV infection (especially types 16 and 18)
  • Smoking
  • Having a weakened immune system (such as from HIV/AIDS)
  • Chlamydia infection
  • Being overweight
  • Long-term use of birth control pills
  • Having multiple full-term pregnancies
  • Young age at first full-term pregnancy
  • Family history of cervical cancer
  • Exposure to diethylstilbestrol (DES) before birth

Symptoms/When to contact a doctor:

Early-stage cervical cancer generally produces no signs or symptoms. More advanced cervical cancer may cause:

  • Vaginal bleeding after intercourse, between periods or after menopause
  • Watery, bloody vaginal discharge that may be heavy and have a foul odor
  • Pelvic pain or pain during intercourse
  • Painful urination
  • Unexplained, persistent pelvic and/or back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue

Contact your doctor if you experience any of these symptoms, especially if they persist for more than two weeks.

HPV Vaccination

The HPV vaccine protects against the virus types that cause most cervical cancers. It's recommended between ages 9 and 26, with catch-up doses through age 45.
