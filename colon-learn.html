Colon Cancer

Starts as polyps Highly preventable

Colon cancer often begins as small growths (polyps) in the large intestine. Screening can find and remove polyps before they become cancer.

What is it?

Cancer in the colon or rectum that can develop from precancerous polyps. Removing polyps early lowers cancer risk.

Stool tests and colonoscopies are both effective; choose the option that fits your risk and access.

Who is most affected?

  • Adults 45+ (risk increases with age)
  • People with family history of colorectal cancer or polyps
  • Individuals with inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's or ulcerative colitis)

Risk factors

  • Family history or hereditary syndromes (Lynch, FAP)
  • Diet high in processed/red meats and low in fiber
  • Low physical activity, obesity, smoking, or heavy alcohol use
  • Type 2 diabetes

Symptoms—talk to a clinician if you notice:

  • Blood in stool or dark/black stools
  • Persistent bowel changes (diarrhea/constipation) lasting more than a few weeks
  • Abdominal cramping or bloating that will not go away
  • Unexplained weight loss or fatigue
  • Iron-deficiency anemia without a clear cause

Prevention

  • Routine screening starting at age 45 (earlier if high risk)
  • High-fiber diet with fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
  • Regular physical activity and maintaining a healthy weight
  • Limiting red/processed meats and alcohol; avoid smoking
